Chess Introduction

What is Chess?

Chess is a widely played sport using a huge range of pieces to outplay your opponent in an exciting test of skill and wits.

Pieces

A wide range of pieces are used to play chess. These pieces have individual points (1-9) and move sets. We will first talk about the king.

The King

The king is what I consider the most valuable piece on the board (although this is debatable) and it is the piece you will worry about the most in most games. You must do everything you can to protect the king. It can move one square in any direction. The king is either worth 0 points, or ∞ depending on who you ask.

The Queen

The Queen is the most valuable piece on the board (although some may say the king is) it can move verticaly and diagonally in any direction. It has a point value of 9.

The Rook

The rook is the most valuable non-royalty piece on the chess board. It can move up, down, left and right as much as it wants, but it cannot go diagonally. It is worth 5 points.

The Bishop

The bishop moves diagonally in any direction as much as it wants, though it cannot go horizontally or vertically. It is worth 3 points.

The Knight

The knight (or horse) moves forward or backwards 2 spaces, then left or right one in any direction. It it also the only piece that can hop over other pieces. Like the bishop, it is also worth 3 points.

The Pawn

The pawn moves up one or two spaces on its first move, then one until it reaches the end of the board. It takes diagonally one space ahead of it in either direction, but not in front. It is the only piece that takes differently than it moves. It is worth 1 point.

Checkmate

In chess, checkmate (often reffered to as "mate") occurs when a player's king is under attack and has no legal moves to escape capture, resulting in the end of the game.

Capturing

In chess, capturing (or taking) is when one colour's piece moves on top of another's, it results in the other colour's piece being captured and removed from the board. E.g. The white queen moves on top of the black pawn, so the black pawn gets captured

Special Rules

There are a few special rules in chess. I will explain them here

Promotion

When a pawn makes it to the other end of the board, it can be turned into any of these pieces:

•A queen

•A rook

•A bishop

•A knight

En Passant

En Passant can be played if your opponent moves their pawn 2 spaces on the first move. If your pawn is now next to it, you can take diagonally

Point Value

Although each piece is worth a set amount of points, you do not score points in chess. These points only exist to keep track of who is in the lead.

Stalemate

Stalemate occurs when someone has no legal moves, but they are not in check. This results in a draw. You should be careful to not accidentally stalemate at the end of the game, as it is easy to do so

Castling

Castling can be played if:

A:The king has not moved

B:The king is not in check

C:The rook has not moved

D:There is nothing in between the king and the rook

When castling, the king moves two places towards the rook it is castling with, and the rook goes on the square right to the king if castling on the left side, and the square left to the king if castling on the right side
